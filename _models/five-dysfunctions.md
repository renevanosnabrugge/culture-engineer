Lencioni's five-layer pyramid explains why technically capable teams fail to deliver, and why fixing the symptom (poor results) without addressing the root (lack of trust) never works.

## The pyramid

```
         Inattention to Results
        Avoidance of Accountability
       Lack of Commitment
      Fear of Conflict
     Absence of Trust        ← root
```

Each dysfunction enables the one above it. The pyramid is sequential — you cannot fix accountability without first addressing commitment, and you cannot fix commitment without honest conflict, and you cannot have productive conflict without trust.

## Layer by layer

**Absence of trust** — team members are not vulnerable with one another. They hide weaknesses, mistakes, and concerns. Every interaction is a performance.

**Fear of conflict** — without trust, teams avoid productive ideological debate. Disagreement goes underground. Meetings become consensus theatre.

**Lack of commitment** — without honest debate, nobody truly buys in. People say yes in the meeting and do whatever they were already planning.

**Avoidance of accountability** — without genuine commitment, nobody holds others to standards they never truly agreed to.

**Inattention to results** — without accountability, individual ego and career concerns take priority over team goals.

## How I use this model

I use the pyramid as a diagnostic tool. When a team is underperforming, I work backwards from the visible symptom (missed delivery, blame culture, disengagement) and try to identify which layer is the actual constraint.

The board always sees the top of the pyramid. The dynamics that produced it have usually been building for years at the base.

## What this model does not cover

It describes the dysfunction, not the repair. Rebuilding trust takes sustained behavioural change by the leader. This is where Brené Brown's work on vulnerability and courage becomes the necessary complement.
