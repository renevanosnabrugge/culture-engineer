ADKAR is a goal-oriented change management model that describes five outcomes an individual must achieve for change to be sustained. The model is sequential — you cannot shortcut an earlier stage by investing more in a later one.

## The five elements

| Stage | Question it answers |
|---|---|
| **A** wareness | Do people know *why* the change is happening? |
| **D** esire | Do they *want* to support and participate? |
| **K** nowledge | Do they know *how* to change? |
| **A** bility | Can they actually *perform* the new behaviour? |
| **R** einforcement | What keeps them from *slipping back*? |

## How I use it

ADKAR makes change problems diagnosable. When a transformation is stalling, you can identify which element is the constraint. Most initiatives fail at Desire — teams understand what is changing and how to operate the new system, but they never genuinely committed because the *why* never landed.

Running an ADKAR assessment at each stage of a transformation gives you a leading indicator of adoption risk before the go-live date, not after.

## Common mistakes

**Confusing Awareness with Desire** — communicating the case for change and assuming people now want it. Awareness is necessary but not sufficient. Desire requires something more personal: a reason the individual cares about.

**Skipping Reinforcement** — declaring success at go-live. Without reinforcement, people revert. New habits require practice and positive feedback to stabilise.
