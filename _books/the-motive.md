There are two reasons people want to lead: the rewards it brings, or the responsibility it carries. Leaders driven by reward treat leadership as a prize — and quietly abdicate the hard, unglamorous work of serving their people. Leaders driven by responsibility do the opposite.

Lencioni's fable follows a CEO who questions his motivation after a difficult conversation with a peer, and gradually realises that much of what he finds burdensome about his role is precisely what the role requires.

## The two motives

**Reward-Centred Leadership** — leadership is primarily a collection of attractive things: status, compensation, influence, being seen as successful. When the less attractive parts arrive (developing people, having difficult conversations, running administrative processes), these leaders delegate them or avoid them.

**Responsibility-Centred Leadership** — leadership is primarily a stewardship obligation. The attractive parts are pleasant. The burdensome parts are still the job.

## Five responsibilities reward-centred leaders neglect

1. Developing subordinates through honest feedback
2. Having difficult conversations about performance
3. Running effective team meetings
4. Managing administrative tasks
5. Communicating important decisions and rationale

## Why this is uncomfortable

Most leaders believe they lead for the right reasons. The book works because it makes the discomfort specific rather than abstract. When you read the list of things reward-centred leaders avoid, it is very easy to recognise yourself — or your peers.
