The companion to The Phoenix Project, told from the developer's perspective. Maxine, a senior developer exiled to a failing project, discovers what separates organisations that ship great software from those that don't.

Kim's Five Ideals describe what distinguishing great engineering organisations from struggling ones:

## The Five Ideals

1. **Locality and Simplicity** — decisions are made close to where the work happens; architecture enables independence rather than forcing coordination.
2. **Focus, Flow, and Joy** — engineers spend their time on creative, value-adding work, not on waiting, re-doing, or navigating bureaucracy.
3. **Improvement of Daily Work** — the organisation invests in making the work better, not just in delivering more work through the same broken process.
4. **Psychological Safety** — engineers can raise problems, admit mistakes, and challenge decisions without fear of punishment.
5. **Customer Focus** — the organisation is genuinely organised around delivering value to the customer, not around internal metrics or politics.

## Why I recommend this alongside The Phoenix Project

Where The Phoenix Project shows the system view — constraints, flow, the operations side — The Unicorn Project shows the developer experience view. Both are necessary. An organisation can have excellent flow from a constraint perspective while still being a miserable place to write code. The Five Ideals name the conditions that make engineering work genuinely good.
