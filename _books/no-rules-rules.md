Netflix removed rules rather than adding them. The logic is counterintuitive but internally consistent: hire only exceptional people, build radical transparency, then extend extraordinary freedom. The result is a culture of high-performing adults who don't need to be managed, just trusted.

Written jointly by Netflix CEO Reed Hastings and cross-cultural researcher Erin Meyer, the book explains how the model evolved — and why it only works in a specific sequence. You cannot extend freedom without first building density of talent and a culture of candour. Doing it out of order produces chaos.

## The three foundations

1. **Talent density** — maintain a team where every person is exceptional. Don't keep adequate performers; they drag down the standard and make rules necessary.
2. **Candour** — create radical transparency so people can make decisions with full information. Secrets are inefficient. Sunshine is cheap.
3. **Freedom** — once talent and candour are established, remove approval processes, expense policies, and holiday limits. Trust people to act in the company's best interest.

## Why I find this model compelling

Most organisations invert this completely. They start with rules to manage mediocre performance and end up with a system that suffocates the exceptional people they actually hired.

The Netflix model has limits — it depends on work that can be evaluated by outcomes rather than hours, and it requires leaders who can give genuinely difficult feedback. But the underlying principle — that adults perform better when treated like adults — is one I believe deeply.
