Daring leaders choose to be seen. They embrace uncertainty, sit with discomfort, and have the difficult conversations that matter. They don't avoid hard feedback or pretend to have all the answers.

Brown's research identifies vulnerability — the willingness to show up without guarantees of outcome — as the core of courageous leadership. This is not soft. It is the hardest thing most leaders are ever asked to do.

## Key ideas

**Armoured leadership vs. daring leadership** — Brown catalogues the ways leaders protect themselves from vulnerability: perfectionism, cynicism, numbing, the need to be right. Each of these armours has a daring alternative.

**BRAVING** — a model for building trust: Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault (confidentiality), Integrity, Non-judgement, Generosity.

**The rumble** — Brown's term for having honest, values-driven conversations about difficult things. Not conflict for its own sake, but the willingness to engage with hard truths rather than avoid them.

## What I got out of this book

I was not an extreme big fan of this book, because I found a bit long and repeating. What I did like is the different approach on leadership. Not results, KPI's or influence strategies but just being yourself, vulnerable and authentic. Psychological safety in organizations is underestimated. There is not many places where real and authentic conversations happen. This only happens, when leaders model the behaviour. Who admit when they don't know something, who invite pushback, who hold themselves accountable publicly.
