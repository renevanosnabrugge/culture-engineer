Former FBI hostage negotiator Chris Voss argues that the rational-actor model of negotiation — find common ground, reach logical compromise — misses the fundamental truth: every negotiation is a conversation about feelings.

Voss spent decades negotiating with people for whom the stakes were life and death. The tactics he developed work precisely because they engage emotional reality rather than trying to route around it.

## Key techniques

**Tactical empathy** — understand and acknowledge the other party's perspective without necessarily agreeing with it. Making people feel understood removes their defensive posture.

**Mirroring** — repeat the last few words someone said. It encourages them to continue and signals that you're listening.

**Calibrated questions** — "How" and "What" questions that give the other party the feeling of control while steering the conversation. "How am I supposed to do that?" is more powerful than "No."

**The accusation audit** — list the worst things the other party might think of you before they say them. It deflates the tension and shows self-awareness.

**No as a starting point** — getting to "No" early creates safety; people who say "No" feel protected and are more willing to engage. "Yes" early in a negotiation is often a false commitment.

## Why I recommend this book

Decision-making in complex organisations involves negotiation constantly — with stakeholders, with peers, with vendors, with teams. The rational approach rarely works as well as it should because the humans involved are not rational. These techniques are immediately practical.
