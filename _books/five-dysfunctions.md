Absence of trust leads to fear of conflict, which leads to lack of commitment, which leads to avoidance of accountability, which ends in inattention to results.

Lencioni's five-layer model — presented as a business fable about a fictional Silicon Valley company — explains why so many technically capable teams still fail to deliver. The pyramid is sequential: each dysfunction enables the one above it, meaning you cannot fix accountability without addressing commitment, and you cannot fix commitment without addressing conflict, and you cannot fix conflict without building trust.

## The five dysfunctions

1. **Absence of trust** — team members are unwilling to be vulnerable with one another; they hide weaknesses and mistakes.
2. **Fear of conflict** — without trust, teams avoid productive ideological debate and replace it with artificial harmony.
3. **Lack of commitment** — without honest debate, people don't buy in to decisions, even ones they appear to accept.
4. **Avoidance of accountability** — without commitment, team members hesitate to hold one another to standards they never truly agreed to.
5. **Inattention to results** — without accountability, individual ego and career concerns take priority over team outcomes.

## Why I return to this model

The model maps almost perfectly onto what happens in engineering organisations under strategic pressure. Boards typically see only the last dysfunction — the platform that shipped late, the team that missed the quarter, the incident that nobody owned. The culture dynamics that drove those outcomes have been building for years through the first four layers.

The practical value of the model is that it gives leaders a diagnostic framework. When a team is underperforming, you can usually trace the root cause to a specific layer of the pyramid and intervene at the right level rather than treating symptoms.

The book is a fast read — a fable format that makes the ideas stick without the density of an academic text.
