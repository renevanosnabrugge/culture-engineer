Every system has exactly one constraint that limits its total output. Improving anything other than that constraint is waste — you make it better but the output doesn't change.

Through a business novel about factory manager Alex Rogo trying to save his plant from closure, Goldratt introduces the Theory of Constraints and the five focusing steps that follow from it.

## The five focusing steps

1. **Identify** the constraint — find the one thing limiting throughput.
2. **Exploit** the constraint — get the most out of it without spending money.
3. **Subordinate** everything else to the constraint — don't optimise non-constraints; optimise for the bottleneck.
4. **Elevate** the constraint — if still a constraint, invest to increase its capacity.
5. **Repeat** — once the constraint moves, find the new one.

## Why this model travels

The Goal was written about manufacturing, but the logic applies identically to software delivery, IT operations, and any knowledge work pipeline. The Phoenix Project explicitly builds on this foundation — the Brent problem in that book is a direct application of Theory of Constraints.

The most useful insight for technology leaders: local optimisation is dangerous. Making individual teams faster without understanding the system constraint often increases work-in-process without improving delivery. You get more output into the queue in front of the bottleneck, not through it.
