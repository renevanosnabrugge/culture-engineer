The leading causes of death in the developed world — heart disease, cancer, type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure — are largely preventable and, in many cases, reversible through diet and lifestyle. Greger's evidence-based case is that whole plant foods, daily movement, and good sleep address the vast majority of chronic disease risk.

The book works through fifteen leading causes of death and presents the best available scientific evidence on dietary factors. It is comprehensive, carefully referenced, and occasionally uncomfortable for anyone who eats the way most people in Western countries eat.

## Why a nutrition book in a technology and culture library

Sustained, high-quality performance requires sustained, high-quality health. The executives and leaders I know who operate at their best over decades — not just in bursts — treat their physical health as a professional asset, not an afterthought.

There is also a consistency principle at work: leaders who ask their organisations to make evidence-based decisions rather than habitual ones should arguably apply the same standard to their own health choices.

I don't recommend this book to everyone. But for people who are ready to take a serious look at what the evidence actually says about food and longevity, it is the most rigorous starting point I know.
