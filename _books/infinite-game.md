Business has no final whistle, no declared winner, no agreed-upon rulebook. Yet most organisations compete as though it does — optimising for quarterly results, reacting to what competitors do, treating leadership as a tournament to be won.

Sinek argues that great leaders understand they are playing an infinite game. The goal is not to win; it is to keep playing. That changes everything about how you make decisions.

## The five essential practices

1. **A Just Cause** — a vision so compelling that people are willing to sacrifice their own interests to advance it. Not a mission statement; a cause.
2. **Trusting Teams** — environments where people feel safe enough to admit mistakes, share problems, and challenge ideas.
3. **A Worthy Rival** — a competitor you respect and learn from, rather than one you obsess over beating.
4. **Existential Flexibility** — the ability to make radical strategic shifts when the Just Cause demands it, even at the cost of short-term performance.
5. **The Courage to Lead** — doing the right thing even when it costs something in the short term.

## Why this applies to technology organisations

Technology organisations are particularly susceptible to finite-game thinking. OKRs, sprint velocity, quarterly roadmaps — all useful tools when held lightly, all destructive when they become the point rather than instruments of a larger purpose.

The leaders who build lasting engineering cultures are playing an infinite game. They invest in people who might leave. They build systems that will outlast their tenure. They sacrifice short-term delivery metrics for long-term capability.
