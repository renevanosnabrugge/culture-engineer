Cross-cultural friction rarely comes from bad values. It comes from invisible differences in norms around communication, feedback, hierarchy, and decision-making. Meyer's eight-scale framework helps global teams decode why their interactions keep going wrong.

Working internationally, I encounter this constantly. A Dutch engineer giving direct feedback to an American colleague, and the American reads it as hostile. A Japanese team saying "that will be difficult" when they mean "no". A German leader expecting a structured decision process while an American peer expects to iterate informally. None of these are failures of goodwill — they are failures of cultural calibration.

## The eight scales

1. **Communicating** — low context (explicit) vs. high context (implicit)
2. **Evaluating** — direct negative feedback vs. indirect negative feedback
3. **Persuading** — principles-first (theoretical) vs. applications-first (practical)
4. **Leading** — egalitarian vs. hierarchical
5. **Deciding** — consensual vs. top-down
6. **Trusting** — task-based vs. relationship-based
7. **Disagreeing** — confrontational vs. avoids confrontation
8. **Scheduling** — linear time vs. flexible time

The insight is that these scales are *independent*. A culture can be very direct on feedback but highly hierarchical in decision-making. You cannot assume one dimension predicts another.

## Why this matters for technology leaders

As engineering organisations become more distributed and diverse, cultural misread is a growing source of friction. This model gives leaders a vocabulary for diagnosing it and a framework for adapting, rather than assuming everyone operates by the same norms.
