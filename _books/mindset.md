The single belief you hold about whether your abilities are fixed or capable of growth shapes nearly everything — how you handle setbacks, whether you seek challenge, how you respond to criticism, and ultimately how much you achieve.

Dweck's decades of research at Stanford distinguishes two orientations:

**Fixed mindset** — talent is innate, effort is for people who lack it, failure is a verdict on your worth, feedback is a threat.

**Growth mindset** — abilities are developed through effort and learning, failure is information, feedback is useful, challenge is the point.

## Why this matters for engineering leaders

The fixed vs. growth distinction shows up everywhere in technology organisations. A team with a fixed mindset around failure will hide incidents, optimise post-mortems for blame deflection, and slowly calcify into a no-risk culture. A team with a growth orientation treats every outage as a learning event and gets faster over time.

The same applies to leaders. Fixed-mindset leaders surround themselves with people who validate them. Growth-mindset leaders actively seek dissenting views and hire people who will challenge their thinking.

The implications for how you structure incentives, run retrospectives, and respond to failure are significant. Culture is downstream of mindset — and mindset is something that can be changed.
