Most HR practices were built to manage compliance, not build great teams. Patty McCord, who co-created the Netflix Culture Deck, argues for a radically simpler approach: treat employees like fully capable adults, tell them the truth, give them real responsibility, and let go.

The book distills the thinking behind Netflix's unconventional people practices — no formal performance reviews, no holiday policy, no approval processes. But McCord is clear that these are outputs, not inputs. The input is a relentless commitment to hiring and keeping people who are genuinely excellent, and then trusting them completely.

## Key ideas

**The feedback problem** — most organisations give feedback too infrequently, too indirectly, and in the wrong context. McCord argues for giving honest feedback in the moment, continuously, as colleagues rather than managers.

**The job of management** — not to develop people for their own sake, but to build a team that can solve the problem the business actually has right now. That sometimes means honest conversations about fit.

**Freedom and responsibility as a pair** — these are not traded off. You cannot have one without the other. Freedom without responsibility is chaos; responsibility without freedom is compliance.

## Why I recommend this book

It challenges assumptions about what HR is *for* in a high-performing technology organisation. If you have ever felt that your people processes were designed to protect the organisation from its employees rather than to enable great work, this book is a useful corrective.
