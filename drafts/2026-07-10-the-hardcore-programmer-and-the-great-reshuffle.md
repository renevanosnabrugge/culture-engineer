A few weeks ago I sat across from a developer who has written low-level systems code for
more than twenty years. The kind of engineer who knows what happens between the lines,
who can read a stack trace like a doctor reads an X-ray. He looked at me and said, almost
quietly, "I am starting to feel like a blacksmith watching the first cars drive past."

That sentence stuck with me. Because he is not wrong, and he is not right either.

The story the market keeps telling is simple and dramatic: AI writes code now, so the
coders are finished. It makes for a good headline and a bad strategy. Coding is not
disappearing. Software still needs to be designed, secured, integrated and maintained,
and someone has to be accountable when it breaks at three in the morning. The machine
does not carry a pager. So no, we are not watching the end of the developer.

What we are watching is something more subtle, and for the people inside it, more
uncomfortable. The disruption is not aimed at the work. It is aimed at the identity that
grew around the work.

## The specialist was the whole point

For decades, the hardcore programmer was the most valuable person in the building
precisely because the knowledge was scarce and hard-won. You spent years learning a
language, a framework, a domain, and that scarcity was your moat. Organisations paid a
premium for it. Entire hiring strategies, career ladders and salary bands were built on
the assumption that deep, narrow mastery of syntax was rare and would stay rare.

That assumption is the thing AI is quietly dismantling. Not the ability to write good
software, but the scarcity of writing it. When a capable model can produce a working
implementation from a clear description, the raw act of translating intent into syntax
stops being the bottleneck. And when the bottleneck moves, so does the value.

This is not the first time our industry has done this to itself. Just like when cloud
removed the person who racked the servers, and containers removed the person who tuned
the individual machines, AI is removing the premium on the pure act of typing code. Each
time, the specialist who defined themselves by the layer being automated felt the ground
shift. Each time, the ones who thrived were the ones who moved up a level, from the *how*
to the *what* and the *why*.

## Where the value actually moves

Here is the part executives need to hear clearly, because it changes how you spend money
and where you place your bets. The value does not evaporate. It relocates. It moves toward
judgment, toward systems thinking, toward the ability to decide what should be built at
all and to stand behind that decision. It moves toward the people who can hold the whole
picture: the architecture, the risk, the compliance boundary, the human on the other end.

That is genuinely good news for your best engineers and genuinely bad news for the way
most organisations still hire and reward them. If your career ladder still pays the most
for the narrowest specialist, you are optimising for the layer that is being commoditised.
If your definition of a senior developer is "writes the most code," you are measuring the
wrong thing at exactly the wrong moment.

The uncomfortable truth is that the great reshuffle of the IT sector is not really a
technology problem. It is a talent and leadership problem wearing a technology costume.
The tools are the easy part. The hard part is helping a twenty-year veteran see that the
blacksmith did not vanish. The blacksmith became the engineer who designed the engine.

## What this asks of leaders

So the question I put back to the people running technology organisations is not "should
we adopt AI." That ship has sailed and you are already on it whether you booked a ticket
or not. The real question is whether your organisation is set up to help people move up
the value stack, or whether it is quietly punishing them for standing where you told them
to stand for the last twenty years.

Because the developer who feels like a blacksmith is watching the cars go past is not
obsolete. He is one of the most valuable people you have, if you give him a reason and a
path to build the engine instead of the horseshoe.

The IT sector is not being replaced. It is being reshuffled. And the organisations that
win the next decade will be the ones that treat that reshuffle as a leadership job, not a
software update.
