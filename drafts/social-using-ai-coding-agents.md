## Contrarian hook

Most CTOs and CIOs I know haven't written a line of code in years. They wear that as a badge of progression.

I think that's a mistake. Not because leaders should become developers again, but because the distance from the craft has a quiet cost: you're dependent on other people's translation, and translations always simplify.

AI coding agents change the equation. Tools like GitHub Copilot's agent mode or Cursor let you describe a task in plain language, and the agent reads the codebase, writes the code, runs the tests, and reports back. You steer. You review. You don't need to remember the syntax.

One CTO I know rebuilt a small internal tool on a Saturday afternoon using Copilot. By Monday he said he understood his team's problems differently. That's not a coincidence. That's what direct engagement with the craft gives you, even if it's just an hour.

The competitive edge isn't in the code you produce. It's in the understanding you rebuild.

## Story format

A CTO told me he hadn't written production code in six years. He said it almost proudly, the way leaders do when they've successfully made the jump from maker to manager.

Then he tried GitHub Copilot on a Saturday. By Sunday evening he'd rebuilt a reporting tool from scratch, alone, in a language he hadn't touched in years.

His words on Monday: "I understood my team's problems in a completely different way."

That's the thing about AI coding agents. They don't ask you to become a developer again. They ask you to re-engage with the texture of the work, at your own pace, with a very capable collaborator alongside you.

The barrier to participation has dropped so dramatically that "I'm not a developer anymore" is no longer a real defence. It's a choice.

## Question format

When did you last write code that actually ran?

Not a script someone handed you, not a no-code workflow. Something you built from a blank file.

For most CTO and CIO roles, the answer is years. That gap matters more than most people admit. When you haven't been close to the craft, you're entirely dependent on your team's translation of what's hard, what's slow, and what's risky. And translations always leave something out.

AI coding agents change this. You describe a task, the agent reads your codebase, writes the code, runs the tests. You steer and review. The syntax problem disappears. The time-commitment problem largely disappears too.

What you get back isn't a second developer career. It's a much sharper antenna for when something doesn't add up, and a genuinely different quality of conversation with your engineering teams.

That credibility gap has been quietly widening. It's now very easy to start closing it.
