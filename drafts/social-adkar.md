## Variant 1: Contrarian Hook

Most change programmes throw training at the problem. Workshops, documentation, onboarding guides. And adoption still stalls six months later.

ADKAR explains why in a single word: Desire.

The model has five stages, in order: Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. They're sequential. You cannot shortcut an earlier stage by investing harder in a later one.

Training is Knowledge. But if people don't genuinely want to move away from what they know, all that Knowledge lands on resistant soil and changes nothing.

In every platform migration and cloud adoption I have been part of, the failure traces back to an ADKAR gap. Almost always, it's Desire. People understood what was changing and knew how the new system worked. They just didn't believe the old way was actually insufficient.

So they sat through the training and went back to their habits.

The fix isn't more training. It's diagnosing which stage is the real constraint, then addressing that — not whatever stage is most comfortable to deliver.

## Variant 2: Story Format

I've seen this pattern more times than I can count. A platform migration, technically solid. The architecture well designed, the documentation thorough, the training programme completed on time.

And then, six months in: the old way was still running in parallel. New features went into the legacy system because that's where people knew what they were doing.

Nobody had asked whether people actually wanted to change. The team jumped straight to Knowledge and Ability, assuming that if you explain it well enough and train people properly, adoption follows.

ADKAR names the stage that was skipped: Desire. Not motivation in the fuzzy sense, but a clear answer to the question every person asks silently — why isn't the old way good enough?

Until that question has a satisfying answer, you can deliver all the training in the world. You are solving the wrong problem.

And honestly, that's a simple diagnostic. It costs nothing to ask.

## Variant 3: Question Format

Why do platform migrations stall after everyone's been trained?

Most of the time, it's not a knowledge problem. People know how the new system works. They sat through the training. They can operate it.

But they don't use it. Or they use it just enough to say they do, then route the real work through the old system.

ADKAR puts a name on what's missing: Desire. The genuine conviction that the old way is insufficient and the new way is worth the discomfort of changing.

Awareness and Desire come before Knowledge in the model, and that sequence matters. You can't train your way past a Desire deficit.

The useful thing about ADKAR is that it makes failure diagnosable. If adoption is stalling, you can usually identify the exact stage that's the constraint and address that specifically, instead of throwing more workshops at a problem that was never about Knowledge in the first place.

What stage is your current initiative stuck on?
