## Contrarian Hook

Most CTOs I know haven't written a line of code in five years. And they wear it like a badge of honour.

I get it. You hire great engineers. Your job is direction, not execution. That used to be a reasonable trade-off.

It isn't anymore.

AI coding agents have changed the re-entry cost so dramatically that "I don't do technical stuff" is increasingly a leadership gap rather than a leadership choice.

You don't need to ship production code. But spending an hour a week exploring your own platform with a tool like GitHub Copilot, asking it questions, prototyping an idea, reading through the architecture you approved six months ago, gives you a qualitatively different relationship with the decisions you make every day.

One CTO told me: "I finally understood what the team had actually built versus what I thought they had built." That's not a productivity win. That's a governance win.

The competitive edge isn't speed. It's judgment.

## Story Format

Before breakfast last week, I spent forty minutes building a prototype with GitHub Copilot Workspace. Not shipping features. Just exploring an idea I had been sitting with.

By noon I had something concrete to show my team, a real conversation starter instead of a slide deck.

That used to take two weeks. A meeting, a sprint item, and a handoff. Now it was a conversation between me and an AI agent.

This is what AI coding agents change for leaders specifically, and it's different from what they change for developers. The developer wins on speed. The leader wins on grounding.

When you stay close enough to the work to have opinions based on evidence rather than intuition, you become a different kind of leader. Better questions. Fewer surprises. A feel for what's actually hard versus what just sounds hard.

Always Be Coding used to be advice for developers who wanted to stay sharp. There's a version of it that applies to leaders now. You just need forty minutes and an AI that will meet you where you are.

## Question Format

When did you last look at the actual code your team is building?

Not a dashboard. Not a status update. The code itself.

If the answer is "a long time ago" or "never, that's not my job", I'd push back gently on the second part.

AI coding agents have removed the biggest barrier leaders had to staying technical: the re-entry cost. You don't need to remember framework syntax or spend three hours setting up an environment. You describe what you want to understand, and the agent helps you explore it.

One CTO told me he used Copilot to finally understand what his platform had actually become after two years of sprint cycles. That kind of grounding changes how you lead, how you ask questions, how you evaluate timelines and trade-offs.

The leaders who will have the clearest instincts in the next five years are the ones who stay in contact with the work, even imperfectly. AI makes that possible in a way it wasn't before.

Are you using it?
