## Contrarian hook

Most technology leaders think stopping coding was the mature move. I'm not sure that's still true.

AI agentic workflows just collapsed the cost of staying technically engaged. You don't need to remember syntax or hold a codebase in your head. You describe what you want, the agent does the heavy lifting, you review what came out.

The reason you stopped, staying current was too expensive, is less valid now. And the cost of staying disconnected is quietly going up.

Teams notice when their CTO hasn't personally felt what a slow CI pipeline or a brittle deployment actually costs. Not in a report. In their hands. It shapes decisions in ways that are hard to see and easy to underestimate.

My suggestion: spend an hour with GitHub Copilot agent mode on a real problem. Something from your actual work. Watch what it does. Where it gets stuck. What it surprises you with. That experience will calibrate your judgment in a way no vendor briefing ever will.

The leaders who will navigate the next wave best aren't the ones with the best AI governance frameworks. They're the ones who've personally broken something with an AI agent and learned from it.

## Story format

A CTO I spoke with a few years ago hadn't written a line of code in six years. He was proud of it. His job was to lead, not to build.

That was a defensible position then. I'm less convinced it is now.

GitHub Copilot's agentic workflows change the economics of staying engaged. The barrier to participation, historically the effort of staying current, dropped significantly. You describe intent, the agent scaffolds the work, you review and steer.

Which means the executive who re-engages now, even briefly, gets something most of their peers don't: a felt sense of what AI-assisted development actually produces. Where it's impressive. Where it still needs human judgment. What to be skeptical about when vendors show you demos.

That calibration is hard to get from a briefing. It's easy to get from an hour with an agent on a real task from your world.

You don't need to be the best engineer in the room. You never did. But right now, staying on the outside of these tools is a choice, and it's one with consequences.

## Question format

When did you last write code? Or even run a script, spin up a pipeline, or watch an AI agent work through a problem end to end?

If the answer is "a few years ago," you're not alone. Most technology leaders let that muscle go as they moved into strategy and leadership. It made sense at the time.

But AI agentic workflows just made re-engagement cheap. You don't need to be current on every framework. You describe the problem, the agent does the scaffolding, and you review what came back.

That hour you spend with GitHub Copilot agent mode on a real task from your work gives you something no slide deck or briefing does: direct experience of what these tools actually produce. Where they're strong. Where they still need a human with judgment in the loop.

The leaders I see navigating this transition well aren't the ones with the best AI strategy documents. They're the ones who've rolled up their sleeves.

Are you one of them?
