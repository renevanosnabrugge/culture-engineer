## Variant 1 — Contrarian Hook

Most organisations invest heavily in tooling and wonder why delivery doesn't improve.

The answer is rarely in the stack.

I've spent years working with software teams on their engineering practices. The pattern I see most consistently: the tools are fine, sometimes excellent. The environment around the tools is where things quietly fall apart. Teams that avoid honest failure retrospectives. Leaders who reward heroic effort instead of steady, sustainable work. Metrics that measure activity rather than outcomes.

You can hand a team the best CI/CD pipeline in the world and they'll route around it if the culture pushes the other way.

Culture engineering is the discipline of shaping that environment deliberately. It's not softer than technical work. If anything, it's harder, and the ROI is bigger.

I finally built a home for everything I've been collecting on this topic: posts, models, book summaries, conference talks and more.

## Variant 3 — Question Format

What actually holds software teams back?

I've been asking that question for most of my career. And after working with enough teams, the honest answer is: it's rarely the technology.

The architecture could be cleaner, sure. The pipeline could be faster. But the real blockers tend to be quieter and harder to fix: the team that can't have an honest conversation about what went wrong, the leadership culture that accidentally rewards heroics, the metrics that measure the wrong things.

That's what I mean by culture engineering: applying the same intentionality to how teams work together that we give to how their systems are built.

I've been writing and speaking about this for years across too many different places. I finally built one home for it.
