## Variant 1 — Contrarian Hook

Most people think software quality is an engineering problem. It isn't.

After a decade of helping teams improve their delivery, the pattern is clear: the bottleneck is almost never the code. It's the environment in which the code gets written. Teams that can't talk honestly about failures. Leaders who reward heroics instead of sustainable habits. Organisations that measure the wrong things and then act surprised when nothing gets better.

Culture engineering is the practice of deliberately shaping that environment. Applying the same rigour we give to software architecture to the human systems that build software.

That's what this site is about. Not another DevOps framework. Not a rebranded agile playbook. A serious look at how you build teams that actually work.

I wrote about this on my blog: https://culture-engineers.nl/blog/2026/07/01/welcome-to-culture-engineers/

## Variant 2 — Story Format

Early in my career I thought the problems were mostly technical. Pick the right architecture, use the right tools, follow the right process.

I was wrong.

The most memorable transformation I worked on had every tool in place. The CI/CD pipeline was solid, the architecture was clean, the process was documented. And yet delivery kept slipping, and nobody talked about why.

The problem was the culture. People were rewarded for looking busy, not for surfacing problems early. That never shows up in a sprint burndown.

After years of working through that pattern with teams across industries, I've built a proper home for these ideas: Culture Engineers. A space to explore the intersection of technology, people, and the practices that bring the two together.

I wrote about this on my blog: https://culture-engineers.nl/blog/2026/07/01/welcome-to-culture-engineers/

## Variant 3 — Question Format

What do you do when your engineering team has all the right tools but still can't deliver consistently?

I've seen this more times than I can count. The architecture is sound, the toolchain is modern, the process is documented. And still, something isn't working. Conversations about problems don't happen. The same mistakes recur. Improvement initiatives stall after the first sprint review.

The answer is almost always the same: the culture needs engineering too.

I've spent years helping teams figure out what that actually means in practice, not in theory. I'm collecting those ideas on my new site, Culture Engineers, alongside presentations, publications, and the books and frameworks that have shaped my thinking.

I wrote about this on my blog: https://culture-engineers.nl/blog/2026/07/01/welcome-to-culture-engineers/
