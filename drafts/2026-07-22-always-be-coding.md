There is a phrase that used to haunt junior salespeople in the 1990s: "Always Be Closing." Relentless, exhausting, and mostly counterproductive. But there is a version of that phrase that actually makes sense for technology leaders in 2026: Always Be Coding.

Not in the way your team codes. Not full sprints, not pull requests that block a release train. But enough to know what is happening in the engine room. And right now, AI coding agents have made that easier than at any point in the last twenty years.

## The gap you stopped noticing

Most technology executives drifted away from hands-on work gradually. First the team grew and the work became coordination. Then the work became strategy. Then the work became stakeholder management, budget cycles, and Powerpoint decks. At some point, the last time you actually built something became a year ago, then three years, then a decade.

That is a normal career path. But it creates a specific kind of blind spot: you are making decisions about systems you can no longer fully imagine. You approve platforms you have never run locally, architecture choices you read about in briefing documents, and tooling investments you hear about in vendor pitches rather than in practice.

The people on your team know this. They adjust what they tell you accordingly.

## What AI coding agents actually do

AI coding agents are not just autocomplete. They are conversational systems you give a task to in plain language, and they write, explain, run, and iterate on code autonomously. Tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and similar agentic environments can take a description of what you want and produce a working prototype, a test suite, a script that queries an API, or a data pipeline, often in minutes.

You do not need to be a developer to use them effectively. You need to be able to describe a problem clearly, read the output critically, and ask good follow-up questions. Those are skills you already have.

This does not mean you will build production software. What it means is that you can again have a direct, unmediated relationship with the technology your organisation depends on. You can experiment. You can verify claims. You can form an opinion based on experience rather than briefing slides.

## Why this matters more for leaders than for developers

Developers will use AI coding agents whether you understand them or not. The interesting question is what happens when their leader understands them too.

First, better conversations. When you have used an AI agent to build something yourself, the conversations about what is easy and what is hard become grounded in reality rather than abstraction. You know which problems yield quickly to these tools and which ones still require careful human judgment. That changes how you scope work, how you challenge estimates, and how you prioritize investment.

Second, faster signal. One of the most valuable things a technology leader can do is notice early when a technology shift is real versus when it is hype. The only reliable way to do that is to use the tool yourself. Reading analyst reports tells you what analysts think. Using the tool tells you what it can actually do.

Third, credibility. And honestly, this matters more than people admit out loud. When you can speak from experience, your team notices. Not because they need you to code alongside them, but because they can hear the difference between a leader who has touched the reality and one who has only read about it.

## What this looks like in practice

This does not require a sabbatical or a return to a developer role. It requires a decision to spend two hours a week on something concrete.

Pick a problem in your organisation that involves data, automation, or analysis. Describe it to an AI coding agent. See what it produces. Iterate on it. Break it, fix it, ask it to explain its reasoning. You will learn more in two hours of this than in most technology briefings.

Some CTOs and CIOs I have spoken with are now using AI agents to build their own personal dashboards: pulling data from their organisation's tools, combining metrics they care about, presenting it in a format that makes sense to them rather than to a vendor's default template. That is not a production system. But it is direct engagement with the technology, and it changes the quality of every conversation they have afterwards.

## The competitive edge is hiding in plain sight

Every organisation is spending time and money trying to understand how AI will change their business. The executives who will navigate that transition best are the ones who are not entirely dependent on others to interpret the technology for them.

AI coding agents have lowered the cost of direct engagement to nearly zero. The barrier is not technical anymore. The barrier is the assumption that this is someone else's job.

It is not. Pick up the tool. See what it does. The gap between strategy and execution has always been partly a gap in understanding. This is one of the cheapest ways to close it that has ever existed.
