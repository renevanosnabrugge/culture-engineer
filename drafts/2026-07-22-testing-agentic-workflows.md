A few years back I was talking with a CTO who had not written a line of code in over six years. He was proud of it. "My job is to lead people, not write software," he said. And for a long time, that was a reasonable position. Leadership at scale means letting go of execution.

I am not sure that logic holds anymore.

## The barrier just dropped

GitHub Copilot, and more specifically agentic workflows where AI completes multi-step tasks on your behalf, has changed what it costs to stay technically engaged. You no longer need to remember the syntax for a GitHub Actions pipeline or hold the entire codebase in your head. You describe the intent, the agent does the scaffolding, and you review the outcome.

That is a fundamentally different offer from what coding required five years ago. The barrier to participation dropped significantly.

Which means the reason most technology leaders stopped coding, it was too expensive to stay current, is no longer as valid. And the cost of staying disconnected is going up.

## What you miss when you stop

There is something that happens to technology leaders who stop engaging with the tools their teams use. The conversations change. Not overnight, but gradually. You start making decisions about velocity, tooling choices, build versus buy, technical debt, without a visceral sense of what those things feel like from inside the work.

Teams notice. When the person steering the organisation has not personally encountered the friction of a slow CI pipeline, a brittle deployment process, or a test suite that nobody trusts, they tend to underprice that friction in roadmap discussions. The engineering culture suffers for it.

This is not about writing production code. It is about staying calibrated.

## Agentic workflows as a re-entry point

Here is what I have found useful: treat agentic workflows not as developer productivity tools, but as a re-entry point for executives who want to stay technically grounded.

Spend an hour with GitHub Copilot agent mode on a real problem. Not a toy example. Something from your actual world: a report you need to automate, a script to extract data from your content calendar, a prototype of a workflow you want your team to build. Let the agent run. Watch what it does. Where does it get stuck? Where does it surprise you?

That experience gives you something no briefing document can: a felt sense of what AI-assisted development actually produces, where it struggles, and what human judgment it still requires. You will leave that session with a much sharper view of what to expect from your teams, what to invest in, and what to be skeptical about when vendors show you demos.

And honestly, it is also just useful. The deliverable is real.

## The competitive edge nobody is talking about

Most conversations about AI and executive leadership focus on governance, risk, and policy. Those are legitimate concerns. But they can become a reason to engage from a distance, to stay in the approver role rather than the practitioner role.

The leaders I see navigating this transition well are not the ones who have the best AI strategy documents. They are the ones who have personally tried to break something with an AI agent and learned from what happened.

There is a kind of authority that comes from direct experience that no slide deck can replicate. When you can say "I ran this myself and here is what I noticed," it changes the conversation. With your team, with your board, and with the vendors selling you the future.

You do not need to be the best engineer in the room. You never needed to be. But right now, with the cost of re-engagement as low as it has ever been, staying on the outside of these tools is a choice. And it is one with consequences.

So, if you have not opened a code editor in a few years: now is a good time to reconsider.
