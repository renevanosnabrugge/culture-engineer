A few years ago, I was in a room with a supervisory board reviewing the results of a major digital transformation program. The programme had been running for three years, consumed tens of millions of euros, and had delivered about 40 percent of what was originally promised. The board wanted to understand why.

The answers they heard were predictable: unclear requirements, scope changes, vendor delays. All partly true. But the real reason was sitting right there in the room, unspoken. The engineering teams had been working in a culture that rewarded output over outcome, punished transparency, and treated failure as a career risk. No process change or tooling investment was going to fix that.

## What Culture Actually Does to Delivery

Engineering culture is not about whether people are happy. It is about the invisible rules that govern how decisions get made, how information flows, how risk is handled, and how teams respond when things go wrong.

In a low-trust culture, engineers spend energy on self-protection. They over-document to cover themselves. They escalate instead of deciding. They avoid ambitious work because the cost of failure is too high. They leave.

In a high-trust, high-accountability culture, engineers move faster because they know what good looks like and feel safe enough to reach for it. They share bad news early. They learn from incidents instead of hiding them. They stay, because the work is meaningful and their growth is visible.

The difference shows up directly in delivery: speed, quality, and cost. This is not a soft claim. The State of DevOps research has tracked it for over a decade. The organizations with the strongest engineering cultures consistently outperform on lead time, deployment frequency, and change failure rate. The gap is not marginal — it is an order of magnitude.

## Why HR Cannot Own This

When engineering culture becomes an HR topic, it gets reduced to what HR knows how to do: engagement surveys, town halls, values workshops, and onboarding programs. These are not bad things. They are just not the things that change engineering culture.

Engineering culture is shaped by org structure, decision rights, incentive systems, tooling investment, and the behavior of technical leaders when it matters most. Those decisions are made by boards, C-suites, and senior management, not by HR departments. When a board approves a cost-cutting round that hollows out the platform team, it has just made a culture decision. When a C-suite tolerates a delivery leader who blames teams publicly, it has made a culture decision. HR cannot undo those.

In other words: culture is a leadership output, not an HR program.

## The Board's Actual Leverage Points

If boards want to influence engineering culture, there are a small number of places where their decisions land directly.

The first is architecture, specifically Conway's Law. The way you structure your teams determines the software they will build. A board that consolidates delivery into a single monolithic programme, with heavy governance and central approval gates, will get a culture of compliance and avoidance. A board that invests in small, autonomous product teams with real ownership will get a culture of accountability and momentum.

The second is what gets measured and rewarded. If the executive incentive structure rewards delivery of features regardless of quality, technical debt will accumulate until it becomes a risk event. That risk event will arrive at the board's door as a security incident, a platform outage, or an inability to change fast enough when the market shifts.

The third is talent: who leads engineering, and what signals are sent about what good technical leadership looks like. If the head of engineering is routinely cut out of strategic decisions, every senior engineer in the organization notices. If technical excellence is rewarded with promotion into management (and nothing else), the best technical people leave.

## The Risk Frame That Changes the Conversation

The most effective way I have seen boards engage with engineering culture is to stop calling it culture and start calling it what it is: execution risk.

When you frame it that way, the questions change. Not "how are our engineers feeling?" but "what percentage of our delivery capacity is blocked by technical debt?" Not "do we have good values?" but "how long does it take to get a change from idea to production?" Not "is our team engaged?" but "what is our rate of unplanned work, and what does that tell us about the health of our platform?"

These are boardroom questions. They have financial consequences. They determine whether a digital strategy actually delivers. And they are answered, ultimately, by the culture that leaders have built, often without realising it.

Here's my take: the organizations that treat engineering culture as a board-level responsibility, not a program that runs in the background of HR, are the ones that close the gap between digital ambition and actual delivery. The others keep commissioning transformation programs and wondering why the results disappoint.
