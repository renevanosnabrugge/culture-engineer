## Contrarian hook

Most CTOs haven't written a line of code in years. And that's considered normal.

I think it's a problem.

Not because technology executives need to merge pull requests. But because every year you don't touch the technology, you become a little more dependent on other people's interpretations of it. You approve platforms you've never run. You fund tools you've never tried. Your team notices, and they adjust what they tell you.

AI coding agents change the equation. You can now describe a problem in plain language, and a tool will write, run, and iterate on code for you, often in minutes. No syntax memorisation. No development environment to configure. Just: here's what I want, show me what you've got.

I spent two hours last month building a personal dashboard with one of these agents. Not production software. Just a thing that pulls the numbers I actually care about, in the format I actually want. And it completely changed the quality of conversations I've had with my team since.

Two hours. That's the investment.

The barrier to re-engaging with technology is no longer technical. It's the assumption that it's someone else's job.

## Story format

A few weeks ago I sat down with an AI coding agent and gave it a task I'd been meaning to hand off to someone for months.

I described what I wanted in plain language. It asked a couple of clarifying questions. Then it built it. Not perfectly on the first try, but close enough that we iterated to something useful in under two hours.

I'm a CTO. My last serious coding project was years ago. And yet: working software, from a conversation.

The thing that struck me wasn't the speed. It was what happened in the team meeting the week after, when someone explained a technical constraint to me. I already knew. I had touched a version of that problem myself, with my own hands. The conversation was different. I asked better questions. I pushed back in the right places.

AI coding agents are not just productivity tools for developers. They are a way for leaders to close the gap between the decisions they make and the technology those decisions depend on.

If you lead technology and you haven't tried one yet, try it this week. Give it something concrete and see what happens.

## Question format

When was the last time you built something with your own hands?

If you lead a technology organisation, that question probably has an uncomfortable answer. The path from practitioner to leader runs straight through "I stopped doing this." First the team grew, then the work became strategy, then the work became stakeholder management and budget cycles. That's normal.

But here's what I think technology executives are underestimating: AI coding agents have made hands-on engagement accessible again. You don't need to be a developer. You need to be able to describe a problem clearly and ask good follow-up questions. Those are skills you already have.

Spend two hours this week giving an AI agent a real problem from your organisation. Something involving data, automation, or analysis. Describe it. See what it builds. Iterate. Ask it to explain its reasoning.

What you'll get is not just a prototype. You'll get a direct, unmediated experience of what these tools can and can't do. And that experience will change every conversation you have with your team about AI investment, tooling decisions, and what's actually hard.

The competitive edge isn't hiding in a vendor briefing. It's hiding in the tool itself.
