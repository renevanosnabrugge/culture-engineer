# My Book Summaries

A curated list of books I've read and summarized — covering leadership, culture, change, negotiation, health, and personal growth.

---

## 🔄 Change & Growth

### ADKAR — Jeffrey M. Hiatt
**A Model for Change in Business, Government and Our Community**

Organizations don't change — people do, one at a time. ADKAR gives leaders a five-step model for guiding individuals through any change: **Awareness** (why change is needed), **Desire** (the personal motivation to support it), **Knowledge** (how to change), **Ability** (to implement the change), and **Reinforcement** (to make it stick). Most change initiatives fail because they skip the human side entirely.

---

### Who Moved My Cheese? — Spencer Johnson
**An A-Mazing Way to Deal with Change in Your Work and in Your Life**

A short parable about four characters in a maze who depend on "cheese" — a metaphor for anything we want in life. When the cheese disappears, some adapt quickly and some resist. The lesson is simple but powerful: the world changes whether we like it or not. Clinging to what was is far more dangerous than moving toward what could be.

---

### The Crossroads of Should and Must — Elle Luna
**Find and Follow Your Passion**

A beautifully illustrated meditation on the life we feel obligated to live ("Should") versus the life we feel called to live ("Must"). Luna argues that everyone arrives at a crossroads between duty and calling — and that choosing "Must" is the only path to a truly fulfilling life, even when it comes with real risk and uncertainty.

---

### The Mountain Is You — Brianna Wiest
**Transforming Self-Sabotage Into Self-Mastery**

Self-sabotage isn't self-destruction — it's self-protection. You undermine what you want because part of you believes you can't have it or don't deserve it. Wiest helps readers understand the emotional and psychological roots of getting in their own way, and offers a path to genuine self-mastery grounded in self-awareness rather than willpower.

## 🧠 Mindset & Psychology

### Mindset — Carol S. Dweck
**The New Psychology of Success**

The single belief you hold about whether your abilities are fixed or capable of growth shapes nearly everything — how you handle setbacks, how hard you work, how you lead. Dweck's research-backed framework distinguishes a **fixed mindset** (talent is static) from a **growth mindset** (talent develops through effort). Cultivating the latter unlocks potential that a fixed mindset silently forecloses.

---

### The Catalyst — Jonah Berger
**How to Change Anyone's Mind**

Pushing harder — more facts, more arguments, more pressure — rarely changes minds. Catalysts do the opposite: they remove the barriers preventing people from changing themselves. Berger's **REDUCE** framework identifies five key barriers (Reactance, Endowment, Distance, Uncertainty, Corroborating Evidence) and offers practical tactics to overcome each one.

## 🎯 Leadership

### Dare to Lead — Brené Brown
**Brave Work. Tough Conversations. Whole Hearts.**

Vulnerability is not weakness — it's the most accurate predictor of courageous leadership. Brown's research shows that most organizations are full of "armored" leadership (perfectionism, cynicism, avoiding hard conversations) which quietly destroys trust and innovation. Daring leaders choose to be seen, embrace uncertainty, and have the difficult conversations that matter.

---

### Start with Why — Simon Sinek
**How Great Leaders Inspire Everyone to Take Action**

Great leaders and organizations communicate from the inside out — starting with *why* they do what they do, not just *what* they do. Sinek's **Golden Circle** model shows that purpose and belief inspire action where features and benefits don't. People don't buy what you do; they buy why you do it.

---

### The Infinite Game — Simon Sinek
**How Great Leaders Build a Team that Keeps Playing to Win**

Business is an infinite game — there's no final score, no declared winner. Yet most leaders play it like a finite one, chasing short-term wins at the expense of long-term health. Sinek argues that truly great leaders have a **Just Cause**, build trusting teams, study worthy rivals, and always prioritize staying in the game over "winning" it.

---

### The Motive — Patrick Lencioni
**Why So Many Leaders Abdicate Their Most Important Responsibilities**

There are two reasons people want to lead: the rewards it brings, or the responsibility it carries. Leaders driven by reward treat leadership as a prize — and quietly abdicate the hard, unglamorous work of serving their people. Leaders driven by responsibility do the opposite. Lencioni's fable exposes this hidden root cause of leadership failure with uncomfortable clarity.

---

### Coach to Coach — Martin Rooney
**An Empowering Story About How to Be a Great Leader**

Through a coaching parable, Rooney argues that great coaches — and great leaders — focus on developing people, not just performance. The best coaches produce great human beings first, and high performers second. The principles apply equally to parenting, management, and mentorship.

---

## 👥 Teams & Culture

### High Ten — Martin Rooney
**An Inspiring Story About Building Great Team Culture**

Culture isn't what happens by accident — it's what leaders build deliberately. A High Ten requires two hands and two people fully present, symbolizing the mutual commitment that great team culture demands. Rooney gives leaders a practical system to create the kind of team people are genuinely proud to be part of.

---

### No Rules Rules — Reed Hastings & Erin Meyer
**Netflix and the Culture of Reinvention**

Netflix removed rules rather than adding them — and it worked, because they followed a specific sequence: hire only exceptional people, then build radical transparency, then extend extraordinary freedom. Each element depends on the others. The result is a culture of high-performing adults who don't need to be managed, just trusted.

---

### Powerful — Patty McCord
**Building a Culture of Freedom and Responsibility**

Most HR practices were built to manage compliance, not build great teams — and they actively get in the way. McCord, who co-created the famous Netflix Culture Deck, argues for treating employees like fully capable adults: tell them the truth, give them real responsibility, and let go of the systems designed to protect the company from its own people.

---

### The 6 Types of Working Genius — Patrick Lencioni
**A Better Way to Understand Your Gifts, Your Frustrations, and Your Team**

Workplace unhappiness usually isn't a character flaw — it's a mismatch between the work you're doing and the type of work you're wired for. Lencioni identifies six genius types that map onto the lifecycle of any project: Wonder, Invention, Discernment, Galvanizing, Enablement, and Tenacity. Healthy teams need all six. Understanding your own wiring changes everything.

---

### The Culture Map — Erin Meyer
**Breaking Through the Invisible Boundaries of Global Business**

Cross-cultural friction rarely comes from bad values — it comes from invisible differences in norms around communication, feedback, hierarchy, trust, and decision-making. Meyer's eight-scale framework helps global teams decode why their interactions keep going wrong and how to navigate them with far more skill and less frustration.

## ⚙️ Operations & Technology

### The Goal — Eliyahu M. Goldratt & Jeff Cox
**A Process of Ongoing Improvement**

Every system has exactly one constraint that limits its total output — and optimizing anywhere else is often wasted effort or actively harmful. Through the business novel of a factory manager racing to save his plant, Goldratt's **Theory of Constraints** turns conventional thinking about efficiency and productivity on its head.

---

### The Phoenix Project — Gene Kim, Kevin Behr & George Spafford
**A Novel About IT, DevOps, and Helping Your Business Win**

IT work behaves exactly like a factory floor — it has flow, bottlenecks, and work-in-process that piles up invisibly. This business novel applies Lean manufacturing and the Theory of Constraints to technology operations, showing how a failing IT department can be transformed by understanding the **Three Ways**: flow, feedback, and continuous learning.

---

### The Unicorn Project — Gene Kim
**A Novel about Developers, Digital Disruption, and Thriving in the Age of Data**

The companion to *The Phoenix Project*, told from a developer's perspective. Maxine Chambers is exiled to a broken team and must fight through bad tooling, tangled dependencies, and bureaucratic indifference to ship code. Kim's **Five Ideals** — Locality and Simplicity, Focus and Flow, Improvement of Daily Work, Psychological Safety, and Customer Focus — describe what separates great engineering organizations from dysfunctional ones.

---

## 🤝 Negotiation & Communication

### Never Split the Difference — Chris Voss
**Negotiating as if Your Life Depended on It**

"Meeting in the middle" sounds fair but usually produces worse outcomes for both sides. Former FBI hostage negotiator Chris Voss argues that real negotiation is about emotion, not logic — and that tactics like tactical empathy, calibrated questions, and mirroring are far more effective than rational compromise. Every negotiation is a conversation about feelings.

---

## 🥦 Health

### How Not to Die — Michael Greger, MD
**Discover the Foods Scientifically Proven to Prevent and Reverse Disease**

The leading causes of death in the developed world are largely preventable. Greger argues that whole plant foods, daily movement, good sleep, and avoiding smoking address the vast majority of chronic disease risk — and that individuals have far more power over their longevity than the healthcare system does. Evidence-based, practical, and occasionally uncomfortable.
