Every week I have a conversation with an engineering leader who tells me their team is
"psychologically safe" — and then describes a team where nobody raises concerns, problems
are hidden until they explode, and retrospectives are a collective exercise in saying nothing.

That's not psychological safety. That's politeness culture.

Let me explain the difference.

## What Psychological Safety Actually Is

Amy Edmondson, who coined the term, defines psychological safety as:

> *"A belief that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas,
> questions, concerns, or mistakes."*

Notice what's not in that definition: agreement, warmth, or absence of conflict.

Psychologically safe teams argue. They surface uncomfortable truths. They say "I think
we're making a mistake" in the middle of a planning session. They tell a senior engineer
their code has a problem. They admit they don't know how something works.

## The Misunderstanding

The confusion usually comes from conflating two different things:

- **Psychological safety**: Can I take this interpersonal risk without being punished?
- **Comfort**: Am I free from discomfort?

A psychologically safe team is often *less* comfortable than an unsafe one, because the
truth is on the table. What changes is that discomfort is productive rather than paralysing.

## Signs You Don't Have It

- Retrospectives where everyone says everything is fine
- Post-mortems that focus on process rather than people (to avoid naming mistakes)
- Questions only asked in private, never in the group
- Junior engineers who defer to seniority even when they see a problem
- Leaders who are never challenged

## How to Build It

Here's what I've seen work:

**Model fallibility.** If you're in a leadership position, share your own mistakes publicly
and matter-of-factly. "I got that wrong. Here's what I learned." This is the most powerful
signal you can send.

**Reward the raising, not just the finding.** When someone flags a risk early — even if it
turns out to be nothing — recognise that behaviour. The alternative is that they stop
flagging risks early.

**Separate the idea from the person.** "I want to push back on this approach" is not an
attack. Create norms where challenging ideas is normal and expected.

**Follow up.** When someone raises a concern and nothing happens, they stop raising concerns.
Close the loop, every time.

---

Psychological safety isn't a soft culture initiative. It's a core engineering capability.
Teams with it learn faster, catch problems earlier, and build better software.

Build it deliberately.
