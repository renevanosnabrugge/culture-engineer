A few months ago, I sat in a strategy session where the question came up: "Should executives be technical?" The usual answers followed. Someone said leaders need to understand technology, not master it. Someone else argued that coding is for engineers. And I kept quiet, because I was thinking about the AI coding session I had done that morning before breakfast.

I had spent forty minutes exploring a new feature concept using GitHub Copilot Workspace. No ticket, no sprint, no handoff. Just a question I had been sitting with, translated into a working prototype I could show my team by noon.

That is a different kind of leadership capability.

## The PowerPoint Trap

There is a comfortable story many leaders tell themselves. "My job is direction, not execution. I set the vision and the team delivers." And in a lot of ways, that is right. You should not be the bottleneck on technical decisions, and you should not be rewriting engineers' pull requests.

But there is a version of that story that quietly becomes a trap. When you stop touching the work entirely, you lose your instincts about what is hard and what is not. You start accepting timelines without knowing if they are realistic. You approve architectures you cannot evaluate. You talk about "shipping faster" without understanding what is actually slowing things down.

The gap between technical leaders who stay in touch with the work and those who drift into pure management is wider than it looks, and it tends to compound.

## What AI Agents Actually Change

AI coding agents, and I am thinking of tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, or the newer agentic coding features, do not just help developers write code faster. They lower the re-entry cost for everyone.

That matters enormously for leaders. The historical barrier to staying technical was time. Learning a new framework, getting a local environment running, context-switching from a governance meeting to a debugging session — that friction accumulated until most leaders gave up entirely. It was not laziness. It was rational prioritisation.

Now the cost structure is different. You can describe what you want to understand or build in plain language, and an agent will scaffold it, explain it, and iterate with you. You do not need to remember every syntax detail. You do not need three hours of setup. You can explore a codebase you have never seen, ask questions about it, and get useful answers in minutes.

This is not about becoming a developer again. It is about reclaiming your ability to engage with the work at a level that makes you a better leader.

## What You Actually Get

When I talk to CTOs and CIOs who have started experimenting with AI coding agents, they rarely talk about productivity metrics. What they describe is something more qualitative: a sense of being grounded.

One CTO told me he started using Copilot to read through his own platform's codebase after a major architectural review. "I finally understood what the team had actually built versus what I thought they had built," he said. That is not a trivial thing. That is the difference between governance based on assumption and governance based on reality.

Another said she used an AI agent to prototype an integration idea she had, just to see if it was feasible. It took her an hour. Before AI agents, that feasibility check would have required a meeting, a sprint item, and two weeks of waiting. Now it was a conversation.

The competitive edge here is not speed. It is judgment.

## Always Be Coding — but Differently

The old "always be coding" advice was aimed at developers. Grind, ship, stay sharp. That version does not translate well to leadership.

But there is a reframed version that does: always stay close enough to the work to have an opinion based on evidence, not just intuition. AI coding agents make that possible in a way that was not realistic five years ago.

You do not need to write production code. But spending an hour a week building something, exploring something, or testing an idea with an AI agent gives you a qualitatively different relationship to the decisions you are making. You start asking better questions. You spot unrealistic estimates earlier. You understand what "technical debt" actually feels like, not just what it costs.

And honestly, in a world where your teams are already using these tools every day, not understanding how they work is its own kind of risk.

So the question is not whether AI changes your role as a leader. It does. The question is whether you are going to engage with that change from the inside, or explain it from the outside.

The inside is more useful.
